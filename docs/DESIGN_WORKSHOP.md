# Vallum Full Design Workshop

## Workshop purpose

Define the complete player experience from first landing through session completion before expanding further chapters.

The workshop must produce accepted decisions, not merely ideas. Its outputs become the design authority for product, UX, game systems, content adaptation, VTT boards, tokens and future companion behaviour.

## Core question

What must the player see, understand, feel and do from the moment they open Vallum until they finish their first Stormwright session?

## Participants and roles

| Role | Responsibility |
|---|---|
| Founder / Creative Director | Canon, emotional truth, product intent and final decisions |
| Lead Game Designer | Loop, pressure, consequences, progression and engagement |
| World Designer / Loremaster | Canon integrity, history, geography, culture and tone |
| UX Lead | Information hierarchy, onboarding, controls and comprehension |
| Level Designer | VTT board composition, zones, movement, threat and spatial storytelling |
| Technical Architect | Board schema, token model, state, persistence and engine boundaries |
| Game Master Designer | Narration, hidden information, consequence delivery and pacing |
| UAT Lead | Acceptance criteria, test paths and release gates |

## Workshop principles

- Start with player experience, not components.
- Preserve uncertainty where the Game Master would hide information.
- Explain the world before demanding knowledge of it.
- Use the board to show pressure rather than repeat text.
- Treat Kael as a person before treating him as a state model.
- Distinguish what the player knows, what Kael knows and what the Game Master knows.
- Make each decision alter at least one of the board, character, account or future possibility.
- Do not accept technical feasibility as evidence of engagement.

## Workshop sequence

### Session 1 — Product promise and emotional target

Decide:

- one-sentence product promise;
- the exact kitchen-table emotional reference;
- solo-first versus party-future boundary;
- what Vallum is not;
- MVP and MLP player promises;
- first-session success definition.

Outputs:

- accepted product promise;
- experience principles;
- exclusions;
- first-session acceptance statement.

### Session 2 — World landing and onboarding

Decide:

- how much of the Stormwright world is introduced;
- what is said about the Compact Lands and Eastern Marches;
- how Kael is introduced;
- what the player is told about the Iron Captain name;
- what remains deliberately mysterious;
- landing-page visual and copy hierarchy;
- accessibility and reading-length limits.

Outputs:

- landing-page content hierarchy;
- final onboarding copy skeleton;
- world introduction boundaries;
- first-time comprehension test.

### Session 3 — Sit-down-at-the-table transition

Decide:

- whether the transition is narrated, animated or both;
- table material and visual mood;
- Game Master position;
- board reveal sequence;
- character-sheet placement;
- sound and silence;
- how long the transition should take;
- whether it can be skipped after first use.

Outputs:

- transition storyboard;
- table composition wireframe;
- sound cues;
- repeat-session behaviour.

### Session 4 — VTT board and level design

Decide:

- board aspect ratio and camera perspective;
- board background asset requirements;
- required scene zones;
- token size and readability;
- active and hidden areas;
- overlay system;
- choice-linked highlighting;
- consequence-linked board changes;
- coded fallback versus image-backed board.

Outputs:

- first ridge board specification;
- board zone map;
- token anchors;
- overlay states;
- level-design acceptance criteria.

### Session 5 — Lead character and token system

Decide:

- Kael token silhouette, material, crest and active state;
- generic class-token pattern;
- named-character versus role-token distinction;
- character-sheet information hierarchy;
- visible versus hidden state;
- how moral portrait is communicated without becoming a stat dashboard.

Outputs:

- Kael token brief;
- token family rules;
- character-card wireframe;
- state-visibility doctrine.

### Session 6 — Game Master experience

Decide:

- GM voice and tone;
- situation and stakes presentation;
- choice-card structure;
- hidden dice and resolution rules;
- consequence placement;
- timing of account updates;
- rules for silence, withholding and revelation;
- error and recovery language.

Outputs:

- Game Master style guide;
- choice-card contract;
- consequence pattern;
- hidden-information matrix.

### Session 7 — Module loop and engagement

Decide:

- standard module phases;
- rising-pressure model;
- number of meaningful decisions;
- state carry and irreversible consequence;
- session-complete structure;
- return hook;
- minimum three-module arc;
- replay and alternate-path expectations.

Outputs:

- module loop;
- Module One scene map;
- cross-module state model;
- return-driver pattern.

### Session 8 — Companion future model

Decide:

- when companions enter the roadmap;
- companion token and sheet requirements;
- personality dimensions;
- intervention frequency;
- conflict, loyalty and trust model;
- AI versus deterministic behaviour boundary;
- player takeover and shared-table future.

Outputs:

- companion foundation specification;
- personality taxonomy;
- safe AI behaviour boundary;
- future multiplayer compatibility requirements.

### Session 9 — Visual system and accessibility

Decide:

- table, board, paper and metal material language;
- colour palette and contrast;
- typography;
- button hierarchy;
- focus and keyboard navigation;
- reduced motion;
- screen-size behaviour;
- audio controls and captions.

Outputs:

- design tokens;
- component hierarchy;
- accessibility baseline;
- visual QA checklist.

### Session 10 — Release plan and signoff

Decide:

- MVP and MLP scope;
- release sequence;
- dependencies;
- risks and deliberate deferrals;
- UAT matrix;
- founder signoff gates;
- archive and continuity updates.

Outputs:

- accepted roadmap;
- delivery matrix;
- UAT plan;
- decision log;
- next release brief.

## Required workshop artefacts

- module loop;

## Workshop completion gate

The workshop is complete only when:

1. the opening experience can be storyboarded without unresolved structural ambiguity;
2. the first board can be produced from an accepted specification;
3. the Game Master and player information boundaries are explicit;
4. the MVP and MLP scopes are accepted;
5. every deferred item has a roadmap destination;
6. decisions are committed to the repository and linked from Obsidian continuity records.
